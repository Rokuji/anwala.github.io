                        <h2>
                            Is there an anti-inflammatory diet?
                        </h2>  
                          <div class="caption margin-24-bottom"><p>Inflammation is part of the body’s immune response; without it, we can&#39;t heal. But when it&#39;s out of control—as in rheumatoid arthritis—it can damage the body. Plus, it&#39;s thought to play a role in obesity, heart disease, and cancer.<br />
                    <br />
                    Foods high in sugar and saturated fat can spur inflammation. “They cause overactivity in the immune system, which can lead to joint pain, fatigue, and damage to the blood vessels,” says Scott Zashin, MD, clinical professor at the University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center in Dallas.<br />
                    <br />
                    Other foods may curb inflammation. Add these items to your plate today.</p></div>

                        <h2>
                            Fatty fish
                        </h2>  
                          <div class="caption margin-24-bottom"><p>Oily fish, like salmon, mackerel, tuna and sardines, are high in omega-3 fatty acids, which can help reduce inflammation. Aim to eat fish several times a week, cooked in healthy ways: In a 2009 study, men who consumed the most omega-3s each day from baked or boiled fish (as opposed to fried, dried or salted) cut their risk of death from heart disease by 23 percent, compared with those who ate the least. Women had a less dramatic drop but were also protected.<br />
                    <br />
                    Not a fan of seafood? Fish oil supplements may help lower inflammation. Also, reduce your intake of omega-6 fatty acids (found in processed foods and some vegetable oils); a healthy balance between omega-3s and omega-6s is essential.</p></div>

                        <h2>
                            Whole grains
                        </h2>  
                          <div class="caption margin-24-bottom"><p>Consuming most of your grains as whole grains, as opposed to refined, white bread, cereal, rice, and pasta can help keep harmful inflammation at bay. That’s because whole grains have more fiber, which has been shown to <a href="http://www.medscape.org/viewarticle/553590" target="_blank">reduce levels of C-reactive protein</a>, a marker of inflammation in the blood.<br />
                    <br />
                    One caveat: Not all products labeled “whole grain” are necessarily healthier than their refined counterparts. To be sure you’re getting the good stuff, look for foods in which the total number of carbohydrate grams per serving is fewer than 10 times the number of fiber grams.</p></div>

                        <h2>
                            Dark leafy greens
                        </h2>  
                          <div class="caption margin-24-bottom"><p>Vitamin E may be key in protecting the body against pro-inflammatory molecules called cytokines.
